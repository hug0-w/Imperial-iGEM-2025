HUMAN PRACTICE

Project Planning
Dr. Lisa Neidhardt
Dr. Oliver Konzock

Early in our ideation phase, we consulted with Dr. Lisa Neidhardt and Dr. Oliver Konzock, both Postdoctoral Researchers with experience in yeast-based chassis systems, including Yarrowia lipolytica and Pichia pastoris. The goal of this meeting was to assess the feasibility of different yeast chassis options within the constraints of the iGEM timeline.

They walked us through the full Yarrowia workflow, from genetic design to transformation, which the full design-build-text cycle takes roughly a month per attempt. When accounting for delays in ordering, shipping, and the exam period for students, this would realistically allow us only two transformation attempts before the competition's final submission deadlines. This raised serious concerns about project risk. Given the high likelihood of delays or failed attempts in biological engineering, relying on only two cycles presented an unacceptably high chance of not generating results. This would compromise the gold medal eligibility and undermining months of work. This consultation resulted in a major design decision. While Yarrowia has great potential, we decided the risk was too high. Following this conversation, we reached out to additional experts to investigate whether Pichia pastoris would allow for a greater number of engineering cycles within the same timeframe. If feasible, this would significantly reduce our experimental risk.

Dr. Lisa Neidhardt and Dr. Oliver Konzock’s honesty gave us the clarity we needed in which chassis to prioritise. Shaping a more time-resilient strategy for the wet lab component of our project.

Dr. Alexander Van de Steen

Continuing our chassis selection process, we met with Dr. Alex Van de Steen, a postdoctoral researcher with expertise in Pichia pastoris. This consultation aimed to clarify the practical timelines associated with Pichia engineering and to evaluate whether it was the most strategic choice for our project goals. Dr. Alex Van de Steen confirmed that Pichia transformation timelines can be compressed to under one month and, with optimisation, may approach the speed of Saccharomyces cerevisiae. However, he also emphasised that unless our project specifically required Pichia’s strengths, such as high volumetric protein yield, absence of self-inhibition, or superior secretion capacity, there was no inherent advantage in selecting it over faster or more extensively characterised organisms like E. coli.

Since our initial concept was to leverage secretion capabilities in growth factor production, this consultation gave us the confidence to move forward with Pichia pastoris as our chassis of choice. Achieving a balance between industrial relevance with practical achievability in the lab.
